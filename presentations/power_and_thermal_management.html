Power & Thermal Management
CONTEXT

Why Power & Thermal Matter

The Scale Problem

  • A single high-end GPU draws 300–700 W under load
  • Multi-GPU racks push 4–6 kW per server
  • Power and heat are first-order engineering constraints

The Opportunity

  • Performance-power curve is nonlinear
  • 10–20% power reduction → only 5–10% throughput loss
  • Quantization cuts energy 30–50%
  • Thermal management prevents costly throttling

Power & Thermal Management — 02
WORKLOAD

Phase-Dependent Power Profile

Prefill Phase

  • Compute-bound — processes full input in parallel
  • Draws peak power from compute cores
  • High GPU utilization, high clock speeds
  • Short duration, intense thermal spike

Decode Phase

  • Memory-bandwidth-bound — sequential token generation
  • Memory subsystem dominates power draw
  • Compute cores underutilized at max clocks
  • Longer duration, sustained thermal load

This variable thermal profile means cooling must handle peak prefill spikes while being efficient during sustained decode.

Power & Thermal Management — 03
SCALING

Power Scaling: Small vs Large GPUs

  • Each chip adds its own idle baseline, VRM losses, and interconnect power
  • Smaller chips run at lower voltages and frequencies
  • Per-chip: 75–150 W vs 300–700 W for large accelerators
  • Lower per-chip thermal density simplifies chip-level cooling
  • But many heat sources complicate system-level cooling

Power & Thermal Management — 04
THERMAL

Thermal Fundamentals

Heat Generation

Every watt consumed → 1 watt of heat. 16 GPUs at 150 W = 2.4 kW GPU heat alone. Full server: 4–6 kW.

Thermal Throttling

Junction temp exceeds 83–95°C → firmware reduces clocks. In distributed inference, one throttled GPU bottlenecks the entire pipeline.

Ambient & Altitude

Data centers: 18–27°C inlet air. Higher temps → faster fans → more power. Altitude: −3–4% cooling per 300m above sea level.

Power & Thermal Management — 06
AIR

Air Cooling for Multi-GPU Systems

Airflow Design Principles

  • Hot-aisle / cold-aisle containment prevents exhaust recirculation
  • Each GPU must receive fresh cool air, not preheated exhaust
  • Blower-style coolers preferred in dense configs — exhaust heat directly out of chassis
  • Open-air coolers create internal hot spots in multi-card setups
Power & Thermal Management — 07
ADVANCED

Liquid & Immersion Cooling

Direct-to-Chip Liquid

  • Cold plates attached to GPU die
  • Closed-loop water / water-glycol
  • Connected to facility chilled water or dedicated CDU
  • 2–3x thermal conductivity vs air
  • Reduced fan power, improved utilization
  • ROI through operational savings despite higher upfront cost

Immersion Cooling

  • Entire server submerged in dielectric fluid
  • Single-phase or two-phase variants
  • Handles >100 kW per rack
  • Eliminates fans entirely — zero noise
  • Growing adoption for AI inference workloads
  • Drawback: hardware extraction needed for service
Power & Thermal Management — 08
POWER

Power Capping & Frequency Scaling

Power Capping via nvidia-smi

  • Set max power below rated TDP
  • 10–20% power reduction → only 5–10% throughput loss
  • Nonlinear curve makes this the most effective efficiency lever

Dynamic Frequency Scaling (DVFS)

  • Auto-adjusts clocks based on workload
  • Decode phase: downclock compute without affecting token speed
  • Prefill phase: ramp up for parallel workload
Power & Thermal Management — 09
TOOLS

Power Management Commands

# Set power cap to 200W (from default 300W TDP)
nvidia-smi -pl 200

# Monitor power, temperature, and clocks in real-time
nvidia-smi dmon -s pcut -d 1

# Query current power and thermal state
nvidia-smi --query-gpu=power.draw,temperature.gpu,clocks.sm,\
  clocks.mem,power.limit --format=csv

# Lock GPU clocks for consistent performance
nvidia-smi -lgc 1200,1800

Key Telemetry

Power draw, junction temp, SM clocks, memory clocks, throttle reason, fan speed

Monitoring Stack

NVIDIA DCGM → Prometheus → Grafana. Correlate with inference latency & throughput.

Quantization as a Power Strategy

Energy per Operation

INT8~0.5x~0.5x

Compound Benefits

  • Fewer bits → less compute energy
  • Less data moved → less memory power
  • Multi-GPU memory-bound systems benefit most
  • Total system power reduction: 30–50%
  • Acceptable quality with modern quantization methods

Quantization is simultaneously a memory, compute, and energy optimization.

Power & Thermal Management — 11
SCHEDULING

Batch-Aware Power Management

Clock Gating

  • Reduce frequency to minimum during idle intervals
  • GPUs idle at 30–50% of TDP
  • Aggressive gating reclaims this waste

Request Batching

  • Consolidate work onto fewer GPUs
  • Power down unused cards
  • Idle timeouts trigger consolidation

Workload Routing

  • Route to already-active GPUs first
  • Wake idle GPUs only when needed
  • Reduces total active GPU count
Power & Thermal Management — 12
INFRASTRUCTURE

Power Delivery Infrastructure

PSU Efficiency

  • 90–96% efficient at 50–80% load
  • Drops at very low or very high utilization
  • Oversizing wastes money; undersizing causes shutdowns

Common Pitfalls

  • Forgetting non-GPU power draw in budget
  • No headroom for prefill power spikes
  • Multiple GPUs sharing PCIe power rails
  • Ignoring PSU efficiency curve

Power & Thermal Management — 15
SUMMARY

Key Takeaways

1. Power Cap Aggressively

The performance-power curve is nonlinear and favorable. 10–20% power reduction costs only 5–10% throughput.

2. Quantize for Energy

Quantization is a triple optimization: memory, compute, and energy. INT4 can cut system power 30–50%.

3. Design Cooling Carefully

Dense multi-card configs need proper airflow. Consider liquid cooling for sustained high-density workloads.

4. Monitor Continuously

Catch thermal throttling before it impacts latency. One throttled GPU bottlenecks the entire distributed pipeline.

Treat power as a first-class design variable to build the most cost-effective and sustainable inference systems.
