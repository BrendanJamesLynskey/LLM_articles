# Google TPU Architecture: Custom Silicon for Transformer Workloads

*March 2026*

## 1. Introduction

Google's Tensor Processing Unit (TPU) represents one of the most ambitious efforts in purpose-built machine learning hardware. While NVIDIA dominates the GPU-accelerated training landscape through broad ecosystem support and CUDA's entrenched developer mindshare, Google has pursued a vertically integrated strategy: designing custom ASICs optimized specifically for the dense linear algebra operations that underpin modern deep learning. From the first TPU deployed in 2016 to the Trillium generation announced in 2024, each iteration has reflected Google's evolving understanding of what transformer workloads truly demand from silicon. For ML engineers evaluating training and inference infrastructure, understanding the TPU architecture is essential to making informed decisions about cost, performance, and long-term platform commitment.

## 2. TPU History: From v1 to Trillium

The original TPU v1, revealed publicly in 2017 but deployed internally at Google since 2016, was an inference-only accelerator. It powered services such as Google Search ranking and the AlphaGo matches, delivering 92 TOPS of 8-bit integer throughput within a 75-watt power envelope. TPU v2 (2017) introduced floating-point training support and the first generation of High Bandwidth Memory (HBM), enabling both forward and backward passes. TPU v3 (2018) doubled the FLOPS of v2, introduced liquid cooling, and scaled pods to 1024 chips connected via a high-bandwidth mesh network. TPU v4 (2022) marked a substantial architectural leap with BFloat16 support at 275 TFLOPS per chip, pods scaling to 4096 chips, and the mature Inter-Chip Interconnect (ICI) fabric. TPU v5e (2023) targeted cost-efficient inference and moderate training workloads, while TPU v5p (late 2023) pushed the performance envelope to 459 TFLOPS with improved HBM capacity and the introduction of SparseCore for embedding-heavy models. Trillium, also referred to as TPU v6e, was announced at Google Cloud Next 2024 and delivers a 4.7x compute improvement per chip over TPU v5e, representing the most significant generational leap in the TPU lineage.

## 3. Systolic Array Design and the Matrix Multiply Unit

At the heart of every TPU lies the Matrix Multiply Unit (MXU), a systolic array architecture that fundamentally differs from the SIMT execution model of NVIDIA GPUs. A systolic array is a grid of processing elements where data flows rhythmically through the array in a pipelined fashion, with each element performing a multiply-accumulate operation and passing the result to its neighbor. In TPU v4, each chip contains two MXUs, each implementing a 128x128 systolic array. This design is exceptionally efficient for matrix multiplications because data is reused as it flows through the array, minimizing memory bandwidth requirements relative to the compute performed. The trade-off is reduced flexibility: operations that do not map cleanly to dense matrix multiplication, such as irregular sparse computations or complex control flow, cannot exploit the MXU effectively. For transformer workloads, however, the attention mechanism and feed-forward layers are dominated by large matrix multiplies, making the systolic array an excellent architectural fit. BFloat16 is the native datatype, offering the same dynamic range as FP32 with reduced mantissa precision, which has proven sufficient for training stability in virtually all large language model architectures.

## 4. TPU v4 Architecture in Detail

TPU v4 chips deliver 275 TFLOPS of BFloat16 performance and are equipped with 32 GB of HBM2e memory providing approximately 1.2 TB/s of memory bandwidth. Each chip contains two TensorCores, each with its own MXU, vector unit, and scalar unit. The vector and scalar units handle non-matmul operations such as activation functions, normalization, and element-wise computations. What distinguishes TPU v4 at the system level is its ICI interconnect, a custom high-bandwidth, low-latency network that connects chips in a 3D torus topology. Each TPU v4 chip has six ICI links, enabling direct communication with neighbors along three axes. A full TPU v4 pod comprises 4096 chips, offering an aggregate of over 1.1 exaFLOPS of BFloat16 compute. This tightly coupled fabric allows collective operations such as all-reduce to complete with significantly lower latency than equivalent operations over host-network fabrics, which is critical for synchronous data-parallel and model-parallel training at scale.

## 5. TPU v5p and SparseCore

TPU v5p pushes per-chip performance to 459 TFLOPS of BFloat16, a 67% increase over v4, while expanding HBM capacity to 95 GB per chip with correspondingly higher memory bandwidth. The most architecturally novel addition is SparseCore, a dedicated processing unit designed to accelerate embedding table lookups and sparse feature interactions. In recommendation models and large-scale retrieval systems, embedding operations can consume a disproportionate share of compute and memory bandwidth. SparseCore offloads these operations from the MXU, allowing dense and sparse computations to proceed concurrently. TPU v5p pods scale to 8960 chips with enhanced ICI bandwidth, making them suitable for training models with hundreds of billions or even trillions of parameters. The improved HBM capacity also reduces the need for aggressive model sharding, simplifying the parallelism strategies required for very large models.

## 6. Trillium (TPU v6)

Trillium represents Google's sixth-generation TPU and delivers a 4.7x improvement in peak compute per chip compared to TPU v5e. Google has emphasized improvements in energy efficiency, with higher FLOPS-per-watt ratios that reduce the operational cost of large training runs. Trillium also increases HBM capacity and bandwidth further, addressing the memory wall that increasingly constrains large language model training. The ICI fabric has been enhanced to support larger pod configurations with improved bisection bandwidth, enabling more efficient collective communication patterns for multi-dimensional parallelism. While full architectural specifications remained partially under NDA at the time of writing, early benchmarks on internal Google workloads suggest that Trillium narrows the per-chip performance gap with NVIDIA's H100 and B200 accelerators, particularly on transformer training workloads where the systolic array architecture excels.

## 7. ICI Mesh Topology vs. NVLink and NVSwitch

The Inter-Chip Interconnect is a defining feature of the TPU platform. Unlike NVIDIA's approach, which uses NVLink for intra-node GPU-to-GPU communication (900 GB/s bidirectional on H100) and NVSwitch for all-to-all connectivity within a node of eight GPUs, TPU ICI provides a uniform interconnect fabric that extends across thousands of chips without requiring a host network boundary. The 3D torus topology means each chip communicates directly with six neighbors, and multi-hop communication follows shortest-path routing through the mesh. This architecture provides predictable, topology-aware collective communication that the XLA compiler can exploit during GSPMD partitioning. In contrast, NVIDIA clusters must transition from NVLink to InfiniBand or RoCE at the node boundary, introducing a bandwidth cliff that complicates scaling beyond a single DGX node. The TPU approach trades peak point-to-point bandwidth for a more uniform and scalable fabric, which tends to favor very large synchronous training jobs where all-reduce latency at scale is the primary bottleneck.

## 8. TPU Pods and Multislice Training

A TPU pod is a collection of TPU chips connected by ICI into a single, tightly coupled compute domain. Multislice training, introduced with TPU v4 and refined in subsequent generations, allows multiple pods (or slices) to be combined for training jobs that exceed the capacity of a single pod. Communication between slices occurs over the datacenter network (DCN) rather than ICI, introducing higher latency and lower bandwidth for inter-slice collectives. The practical implication is that parallelism strategies must be carefully designed: data parallelism and pipeline parallelism typically span slices, while tensor parallelism remains within a single slice where ICI provides sufficient bandwidth. Google's Pathways system and GSPMD compiler handle much of this partitioning automatically, but ML engineers must still reason about the placement of model components to minimize cross-slice communication. Multislice configurations have been used to train models such as Gemini and PaLM, demonstrating that the approach scales to tens of thousands of chips.

## 9. Software Stack: JAX, XLA, and GSPMD

The TPU software stack is built around JAX as the primary programming model and XLA (Accelerated Linear Algebra) as the compiler backend. JAX provides a NumPy-like API with automatic differentiation and just-in-time compilation through XLA. When a JAX function is JIT-compiled, XLA generates an HLO (High-Level Operations) graph that is then optimized and lowered to TPU machine code. GSPMD (General and Scalable Parallelization for ML Computation Graphs) is the compiler pass responsible for automatically partitioning computations and tensors across devices according to user-specified sharding annotations. Engineers annotate tensors with logical mesh dimensions, and GSPMD inserts the necessary collective operations and resharding instructions. This declarative approach to parallelism is arguably more ergonomic than manually writing FSDP or tensor-parallel code in PyTorch, but it requires understanding the sharding propagation rules and XLA's optimization behavior. Pax and MaxText are reference frameworks built on JAX that provide battle-tested implementations of transformer training pipelines on TPUs.

## 10. Inference on TPUs

While TPUs are most associated with training, Google has invested heavily in TPU-based inference, particularly for serving its own products. Cloud TPU inference offers autoscaling TPU endpoints through Vertex AI and GKE, supporting both continuous batching and dynamic batching strategies. TPU v5e was specifically designed with inference cost-efficiency in mind, offering a lower price point per chip while maintaining sufficient compute for serving large language models. Optimizations include int8 quantization support, KV-cache management tuned to HBM capacity, and speculative decoding. For models trained natively on TPUs using JAX, the deployment path is streamlined because the same XLA compilation pipeline handles both training and inference graphs. SAX (Serving on Accelerators with XLA) is Google's serving framework that manages model sharding across TPU chips, request batching, and load balancing. Recent improvements have brought TPU inference latency competitive with TensorRT-LLM on equivalent NVIDIA hardware for common LLM serving scenarios.

## 11. Comparison with NVIDIA GPUs

Comparing TPUs to NVIDIA GPUs requires examining multiple dimensions beyond peak FLOPS. An H100 SXM delivers 989 TFLOPS of BFloat16 with sparsity (495 TFLOPS dense), compared to TPU v5p's 459 TFLOPS. However, NVIDIA's FLOPS advantage is partially offset by the TPU's more efficient systolic array utilization and superior interconnect scaling. Memory bandwidth favors H100 at 3.35 TB/s versus approximately 2.4 TB/s for TPU v5p, which matters for memory-bound inference workloads. On cost, Google has historically priced TPUs aggressively: on-demand TPU v5e pricing undercuts equivalent A100 pricing on a per-FLOP basis, though direct comparisons are complicated by ecosystem differences. The B200 generation from NVIDIA raises the bar further with 4.5 petaFLOPS per rack and second-generation transformer engines. Ultimately, the choice often depends less on raw silicon performance and more on software ecosystem maturity, team expertise, and deployment flexibility.

## 12. TPU Availability and Access

TPUs are available exclusively through Google Cloud Platform. On-demand TPU VMs can be provisioned for short experiments, while reserved and committed-use contracts offer significant discounts for sustained workloads. TPU pods are accessed as GKE (Google Kubernetes Engine) node pools or through Queued Resources for batch-style training jobs. Google's AI Hypercomputer packaging bundles TPU slices with orchestration, storage, and networking into a managed training platform. Unlike NVIDIA GPUs, which are available from every major cloud provider and on-premises, TPUs represent a single-vendor, single-cloud commitment. This has implications for negotiating leverage, disaster recovery, and multi-cloud strategies that engineering leaders must weigh carefully.

## 13. Limitations and Considerations

The most significant limitation of the TPU platform is ecosystem lock-in. JAX and TensorFlow are first-class citizens; PyTorch support via PyTorch/XLA has improved substantially but still lags behind native CUDA PyTorch in operator coverage, debugging ergonomics, and community library compatibility. Models that rely on custom CUDA kernels, such as FlashAttention or many efficient attention variants, require reimplementation in Pallas (the TPU kernel language) or must wait for XLA-level equivalents. Deployment is constrained to GKE, limiting integration with existing on-premises or multi-cloud infrastructure. Debugging performance issues requires familiarity with XLA profiling tools and understanding of HLO graph transformations, which presents a steeper learning curve than NVIDIA's Nsight ecosystem. Finally, while Google's internal teams have demonstrated extraordinary scale on TPUs, the external community of TPU practitioners remains smaller, resulting in fewer public training recipes, tutorials, and battle-tested configurations compared to the CUDA ecosystem.

## 14. Conclusion

Google's TPU architecture represents a compelling alternative to NVIDIA GPUs for large-scale transformer training and inference. The systolic array design delivers exceptional efficiency on the dense matrix operations that dominate modern LLM workloads, while the ICI interconnect enables scaling to thousands of chips without the bandwidth discontinuities inherent in GPU cluster networking. The JAX/XLA software stack provides powerful abstractions for distributed training, and the cost structure can be favorable for teams willing to commit to the Google Cloud ecosystem. However, the narrower software ecosystem, PyTorch compatibility gaps, and single-vendor dependency are real constraints that must be evaluated against each organization's technical requirements and strategic priorities. As Trillium and future generations continue to close the raw performance gap with NVIDIA's latest offerings, the TPU platform's differentiation will increasingly rest on its system-level integration, compiler intelligence, and the total cost of ownership for sustained, large-scale ML workloads.
