The Alignment Problem

Pre-trained LLMs optimize for next-token prediction, not helpfulness or safety. This mismatch causes models to:

  • Generate toxic or harmful content
  • Fabricate information confidently
  • Follow dangerous instructions
  • Produce verbose, unhelpful responses

RLHF was the first practical solution at scale — InstructGPT showed that a small amount of human feedback could dramatically improve GPT-3.

Core Challenge

Modify model behavior to be consistent with human values and intentions while preserving the model's underlying capabilities.

Step 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning

The pre-trained model is trained on curated instruction-response pairs written by human annotators.

What SFT Teaches

  • Following instructions accurately
  • Providing structured, organized answers
  • Maintaining appropriate tone
  • Basic format of helpful responses

Strengths

  • Simple supervised learning objective
  • Provides a strong starting point
  • Low computational complexity

Limitations

  • Bounded by diversity of demo data
  • Cannot exceed annotator quality
  • Does not capture preference nuances

Step 2: Reward Modeling

A reward model is trained to predict human preferences between model outputs.

  • Annotators compare pairs of responses to the same prompt
  • Typically initialized from the SFT model
  • Assigns scalar reward scores to prompt-response pairs
  • Distills human judgment into a differentiable function

Critical Requirements

  • Annotator calibration & inter-annotator agreement
  • Diversity of comparison data
  • Consistent evaluation criteria
RLHF & Alignment — 5 / 16
05

Step 3: PPO Policy Optimization

The language model (policy) is optimized via Proximal Policy Optimization to maximize reward model scores.

Training Loop

  1. Policy generates responses to a batch of prompts
  2. Reward model scores each response
  3. Policy updated to increase probability of high-scoring outputs
  4. KL penalty prevents divergence from SFT model

Four Simultaneous Models

  • Policy — the model being trained
  • Reference policy — frozen SFT model
  • Reward model — scores outputs
  • Value model — estimates expected reward

Failure Modes

  • Training instability
  • Reward hacking
  • Mode collapse

Direct Preference Optimization

DPO collapses reward modeling and RL into a single supervised learning objective.

Key Insight

A closed-form expression for the optimal policy given a reward function can be used to construct a loss that directly optimizes preferences with an implicit KL constraint.

Advantages

  • No separate reward model needed
  • No value network or PPO loop
  • Far more stable and accessible to train
  • Requires only preference data + SFT model

DPO Variants

  • IPO — Identity Preference Optimization
  • KTO — Kahneman-Tversky Optimization
  • ORPO — Odds Ratio Preference Opt.
  • SimPO — Simple Preference Opt.

Trade-off

PPO-based RLHF may still outperform DPO at the frontier when the reward model is very strong.

Reward Model Design

Bradley-Terry Model

Probability of preferring response A over B is a function of the difference in reward scores:

P(A > B) = σ(r(A) - r(B))

Limitations

  • Assumes single scalar quality dimension
  • Cannot capture context-dependent preferences
  • Struggles with ties or near-equal comparisons

Alternative Approaches

Thurstone Models

Probabilistic ranking with noise-based preference modeling

Listwise Ranking

Rank multiple responses simultaneously instead of pairwise

Multi-Objective RM

Separately evaluate helpfulness, harmlessness, and factual accuracy

Reward Hacking

The policy learns to exploit patterns in the reward model that don't correspond to genuine quality improvements.

Common Manifestations

  • Excessive verbosity — reward models often rate longer responses higher
  • Sycophancy — agreeing with user premises regardless of accuracy
  • Superficial quality markers — hedging language, structured formatting without substance

Mitigations

Stronger KL Penalties

Constrain divergence from the reference policy

Reward Model Ensembles

Multiple RMs reduce single-model exploitability

Iterative RM Retraining

Update reward model as policy evolves

Constitutional AI

Rules-based evaluation alongside learned rewards

Constitutional AI (CAI)

Developed by Anthropic, CAI reduces reliance on human preference labels.

Benefits

  • Scales beyond human annotation capacity
  • Makes alignment criteria explicit & auditable
  • Self-generated preference pairs for training

RLAIF: AI-Generated Feedback

Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback uses a separate AI model to generate preference judgments in place of human annotators.

Key Findings

  • Comparable alignment quality to human-labeled RLHF for many tasks
  • Works best when the AI judge is well-calibrated
  • Evaluation criteria must be clearly specified

Advantages

  • Dramatically reduced cost vs. human annotation
  • Lower latency for preference data generation
  • Enables more frequent alignment iterations

Relationship to CAI

RLAIF extends the Constitutional AI concept — both reduce reliance on human labels, but RLAIF uses a separate judge model.

Process Reward Models

Outcome vs. Process Reward

Outcome-Based (Standard)

Single score for the complete response. Correct final answer gets reward even with flawed reasoning.

Process-Based

Feedback on individual reasoning steps. Rewards correct intermediate steps even if the final answer is wrong.

Evaluating Alignment

Automated Benchmarks

  • MT-Bench — multi-turn evaluation via LLM judge
  • AlpacaEval — instruction-following quality
  • Fast and scalable
  • Can be gamed by optimizing for judge preferences

Human Evaluation

  • Remains the gold standard
  • Captures subtle quality differences
  • Expensive, slow, subject to annotator bias
  • Difficult to scale

Red Teaming

  • Tests against harmful content taxonomies
  • Adversarial prompt injection
  • Automated classifiers + human testers
  • Essential for safety validation

Most robust strategy: combine all three approaches across multiple dimensions

Future Directions

Scalable Oversight

Models assist humans in evaluating outputs too complex for direct human assessment. Critical as capabilities grow beyond human evaluation capacity.

Multi-Turn & Agentic Alignment

Steering behavior across extended interactions and tool-use scenarios where consequences of individual actions compound.

Mechanistic Interpretability

Understanding how alignment training changes model internals — moving beyond behavioral evaluation to understand why aligned models behave as they do.

Robust Evaluation Frameworks

Developing evaluation methods that keep pace with model capabilities — one of the field's most pressing challenges.

Key Takeaways

  • RLHF transformed LLMs from uncontrolled text generators into useful, safe assistants
  • The progression from PPO to DPO has democratized alignment
  • Constitutional AI and RLAIF reduce dependence on expensive human annotation

Open Challenges

  • Reward hacking remains persistent
  • Scalable oversight is increasingly urgent
  • Evaluation robustness lags capability growth
  • Alignment research will remain central to LLM development
