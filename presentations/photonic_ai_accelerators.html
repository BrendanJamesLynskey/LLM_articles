CONTEXT

Why Compute With Light

Every commercial AI accelerator today moves electrons through transistors. A small but well-funded set of companies is building accelerators that compute with light instead.

Energy

  • No resistive heating in waveguides
  • Optical interconnect <1 pJ/bit
  • Electrical 5–10 pJ/bit
  • 5–10× energy advantage

Parallelism

  • Multiple wavelengths in same waveguide (WDM)
  • Native parallel data paths
  • Matrix mul through interference

Speed

  • Light-speed data movement
  • Distance-independent latency
  • Picosecond compute through interferometers

PHYSICS

Photonic Matrix Multiplication

The Mechanism

  • Encode vector as amplitudes of N wavelengths or N waveguides
  • Pass through grid of Mach-Zehnder interferometers
  • MZIs implement arbitrary unitary transformations
  • Output detected by photodetector array
  • Whole computation in tens of picoseconds

Topologies

  • Reck or Clements scheme for arbitrary N×N unitaries
  • Limited by chip area to ~64×64
  • Larger matrices via tiling

The Win

Operation completes at the speed of light through the device, independent of matrix size up to the chip-area limit.

CHALLENGES

The Catches

Conversion Costs

  • Inputs come from electronic memory
  • DAC + modulator on input side
  • Photodetector + ADC on output side
  • Energy and latency at every E/O transition
  • Wins amortize on large operations only

Precision

  • Photonic compute is analog
  • 6–8 bits effective precision typical
  • OK for inference (post-quantization)
  • Insufficient for training

Manufacturing

  • Silicon photonics processes much less mature than CMOS
  • Few foundries, lower yields
  • Hybrid integration with electronics is hard

Programming

  • No CUDA equivalent
  • Each vendor has its own toolchain
  • No portability between products

FIT

Where It Wins

Good Fits

  • Large transformer inference (matmul-dominated)
  • Edge AI with strict power budgets
  • Optical interconnect for conventional accelerators
  • Recommendation systems with large embeddings

Bad Fits

  • Training (precision)
  • Heavy control flow / branching
  • Small or sparse operations
  • Workloads with frequent E/O transitions

The Pragmatic Path

Ayar Labs is the most likely first widespread success: it doesn’t try to do compute in light, only data movement. That’s a more conservative bet that fits naturally into the existing chip ecosystem — conventional electronic accelerators, faster optical interconnects.

ECON

The Energy Argument

The Demand Driver

  • AI inference may consume meaningful fractions of global electricity
  • Per-token cost is a growing economic and environmental concern
  • 10× efficiency gain would be transformative
  • Photonics has a credible path

The Wager

  • Maturity curve must catch electronics’ steady gains
  • Conversion overhead must drop
  • Manufacturing must scale
  • Programming model must converge

WATCHERS

Why Hyperscalers Care

The Investment

  • Google has acquired multiple silicon photonics teams
  • Microsoft has internal photonic computing research
  • Meta investing in optical interconnects for datacenters
  • AWS believed to have a quiet photonic strategy

The Bet

AI workloads are growing faster than Moore’s Law. Conventional electronic improvements are not keeping pace. If a 10× efficiency improvement is possible from any direction, the hyperscalers want to be in position to exploit it. Photonics is one of the few credible paths.

SUMMARY

Worth Watching

What’s Real

  • Physics is genuine; energy advantages are large
  • First products are shipping in limited volumes
  • Optical interconnect path is the most likely first mainstream success
  • Programming model is the largest non-physics barrier

Practitioner’s Lesson

For most teams, photonic accelerators are not deployable today and not worth investing in software portability for. For research teams and frontier infrastructure builders, worth tracking. The first product to deliver on the energy promise at competitive pricing will reshape inference economics.
