The Core Problem

Infrastructure planning assumes GPU power is roughly constant near TDP. Reality is far more complex.

Tripped Breakers

Correlated power spikes across GPU groups can exceed PSU ratings in milliseconds

Wasted Capacity

Over-provisioning for worst-case peaks leaves 15–30% of power infrastructure idle

Performance Cliffs

Thermal throttling from power bursts causes unpredictable latency spikes

Power fluctuates on timescales from microseconds (kernel launches) to hours (shifting traffic), spanning the full range from 30–50% TDP (idle) to spikes exceeding nominal TDP.

Thermal Feedback & Path Dependence

Path Dependence

Power at time T depends on thermal history, not just current workload. A cold start draws less power than the same workload after sustained computation.

Thermal Control Response

  • Increase fan speed (draws more system power)
  • Throttle clocks (reduces dynamic power + performance)

Hysteresis Effect

Same workload, different power draw — depending on what happened minutes or hours ago.

Training: Sources of Variability

Gradient Sync Bubbles

  • GPUs idle waiting for stragglers
  • Drop to near-idle power within 1 ms
  • Spike back to full when sync completes
  • Bubble size is stochastic, varies step-to-step

Data-Dependent Compute

  • Variable sequence lengths within batches
  • MoE routes different tokens to different experts
  • Sparse attention activates variable compute
  • Power varies between identical training steps

Checkpointing

  • Shifts from GPU compute to storage I/O
  • GPU power drops sharply
  • Storage + network power increases
  • Abrupt, scheduled transitions

Evaluation Runs

  • Different batch sizes than training
  • No gradient computation
  • Different memory access patterns
  • Distinctly different power profile

Inference: Request & Batching Dynamics

Bursty Arrivals

Nearly idle → dozens of simultaneous requests. Each triggers a prefill spike. Power is a complex function of how many requests are in each phase.

Variable Sequences

50-token prompt: brief, small spike. 10K-token prompt: extended high-power burst. Distribution shifts with user behavior throughout the day.

Continuous Batching

Batch composition changes every decode iteration. Effective batch size fluctuates → utilization & power fluctuate. Speculative decoding adds further variability.

Quantization Effects

  • FP16 vs INT8 vs INT4 use different data paths
  • Mixed-precision creates heterogeneous power within a single pass
  • Dynamic precision selection shifts profiles under load

KV-Cache Operations

  • PagedAttention → irregular memory access patterns
  • Cache eviction triggers extra memory ops
  • KV-cache offloading shifts power between GPU and host

Multi-GPU: Synchronized Spikes

Tensor Parallelism

All GPUs execute the same computation simultaneously. A prefill triggers 8x single-GPU spike concentrated in one instant.

Worst Case for PSUs

Correlated spikes can trigger power supply overload protections if PSU is not rated for aggregate peak.

Desynchronization & Thermal Cascades

Pipeline Parallelism / Multi-Tenant

  • Different GPU groups serve different requests
  • One group in prefill, another in decode
  • Partially averages out spikes at system level
  • Smoothing is statistical and unreliable
  • Coincident spikes still occur under bursty traffic

Thermal Cascade

  • GPU 0's heat raises ambient around GPU 1
  • GPU 1's leakage rises — even if its workload is unchanged
  • May trigger GPU 1's thermal throttling
  • Power & performance coupled to neighbor's history

PSU Efficiency Variation

Efficiency varies with load: 96% at 50% load, 91% at 10% load. Wall power fluctuates more than GPU power alone.

Why Static Power Models Fail

Utilization Is Not a Scalar

nvidia-smi measures fraction of time any kernel runs — not what it does. Two workloads at "95% utilization" can differ by 2x in power.

Phase Transitions Are Abrupt

Prefill → decode can change power by hundreds of watts in 1 ms. Time-averaged measurements miss these transients.

Workload Is Externally Driven

Inference depends on user requests: arrival times, prompt lengths, content. None under system control; statistics shift throughout the day.

Thermal History = Hysteresis

Same workload, different power — depending on what the GPU was doing minutes or hours ago.

Multi-GPU Interactions Are Emergent

Correlated spikes, thermal coupling, PCIe contention, network congestion — arise from interaction of components, not any single component in isolation.

Strategy: Power Capping

How It Works

nvidia-smi -pl <watts>

Sets a ceiling below TDP. Compresses the power distribution into a narrower range. Acts as a variance reducer, not just a power reducer.

The Sweet Spot

For memory-bound decode workloads: a 20% power cap reduction may cost only 5–10% throughput while dramatically reducing peak-to-average ratio.

Strategy: Scheduling & Disaggregation

Staggered Scheduling

  • Deliberately offset prefill starts across GPU groups
  • GPUs 0–3 prefill, then 4–7 after a delay
  • Trades small latency for major peak reduction
  • Implementable at load balancer level

Disaggregated Prefill/Decode

  • Separate GPU pools for each phase
  • Prefill pool: consistently high power
  • Decode pool: consistently moderate power
  • Both have lower variance than mixed

Statistical Multiplexing

  • Provision for 99th-pct aggregate power, not absolute worst case
  • Requires continuous monitoring + alerting
  • Can reduce infra costs by 15–30%

Frameworks: Splitwise DistServe support disaggregated serving natively.

Strategy: Thermal Preconditioning

The Idea

Keep GPUs at a moderate, stable temperature rather than cycling between cold idle and hot peak. This reduces leakage variability and makes power trajectories more predictable.

Implementation

  • Maintain low-priority background workload during idle
  • Set a minimum clock floor
  • Smaller thermal transient when real work arrives
  • More predictable power trajectory

Key Takeaways

Power Is Dynamic, Not Static

It varies with kernel type, thermal history, request patterns, and multi-GPU interactions. A TDP number is not a plan.

Provision for Statistical Peak

Size infrastructure for observed 99th-pct aggregate power — not average (underflows) or absolute worst case (wasteful).

Measure Your Workload

The actual power distribution of your specific workload on your specific hardware is the ground truth. Theoretical models are insufficient.

Reduce Variance, Not Just Mean

Power capping, staggered scheduling, and disaggregated serving all compress the power distribution — making planning tractable.

Desynchronize Spikes

Staggered prefills, pipeline parallelism, and multi-tenant serving smooth aggregate power at the system and rack level.

The Bottom Line

Understanding power as a dynamic, workload-dependent variable is the first step to infrastructure that is both reliable and cost-efficient.
